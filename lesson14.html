Phone calls

In today's lesson we will talk about phone etiquette. How to answer the phone, how to take a message and so on. Without further ado, let's get right into it!

call - llamar

The verb "call" can mean two things. "Call someone on the phone" (Llamar a alguien por teléfono) or "Call someone by their name" (Llamar a alguien por su nombre). We will talk in depths about this word, for now let's just focus on the verb "call". In spanish there is a way to ask a name of some object that we don't know the name of - "Como se dice?". In English we have a way to ask the same thing which involves the word "call". The phrase is "what do you call it?". It's important to note that we use the word "what" not "how". When we need a name of a person we ask "what is your name?". The same logic applies here. We're also using the pronoun "you". it's another way to ask about something using a neutral pronoun. "You", in this context is a neutral pronoun. We're not adressing a person we're talking to, we're asking what people, in general, call something.

What do you call it? - Como se dice?

The word "call" is also a noun which means "llamada". Usually you use when someone calls you. You might remember from the previous lessons that the verb "get" means "recibir", or rather it's one of its meanings

I got a call - Recibí una llamada
I got a call from Mark - Recibí una llamada de Mark
Give me a call - Llámame

If somebody called us, and we call them back we use "call back" (Devolver la llamada)

I will call you back - Te devolveré la llamada
Call back later - Devuelve la llamada más tarde

When we call someone we usually say "hello" when they pick up. If the call was taken by another person we can ask them if we can talk to the person we were calling

Can i talk to Mark? - ¿Puedo hablar con Mark?

If you were told that you can't talk to the person you're calling, the person on the phone might ask you if you'd like to leeave a message. if they don't do it you can ask if you can leave a message yourself

take - tomar
leave - dejar

Can i take a message? - ¿Puedo tomar un mensaje?
Can i leave a message? - ¿Puedo dejar un mensaje?
Please ask him to call me back - Por favor, pídele que me devuelva la llamada

There are other forms of communication like texting and emails. In order to ask someone to text you we say "text me". "Text" can be used as a verb. When we want someone to send us an email we can use the word "email" as a verb as well "Email me"

Text me - Envíame un mensaje
Email me - Envíame un correo electrónico

"Who" as a subject and an object

Words like "who" or "what" can be a subject or an objects in a sentence depending on its structure. I think for this one, the best way to explain it is by showing some examples, so that you can recongnize the pattern

Who called you? - ¿Quién te llamó?
Who did you call? - ¿A quién llamaste?

So when we say "who talked to you?" "who" is a subject, as it is performing the action talking. In "Who did you talk to?" "you" is the subject who was performing the action, and "who" is a subject as it is someone who is recieving the action. Let's take a look at another one

Who sent this letter? - ¿Quién envió esto?
Who did you send? - ¿A quién enviaste?

In the first "who" is an object who did trhe sending of the letter, in the second one "who" is an object, a person that was sent somewhere by "you". There would be a similar structure with the word "what" - "what did you send?" (¿Qué enviaste?). With "what" we are asking about an object that was sent, with "who" we are asking about the person that was sent

Who saw you? - ¿Quién te vio?
Who did you see? - ¿A quién viste?

"For" at the end of a sentence

En inglés, a veces se coloca la preposición al final de la oración, especialmente en preguntas informales. Esto es diferente al español, donde normalmente se coloca la preposición antes del pronombre interrogativo.

Who did you do it for? - ¿Para quién lo hiciste?

En inglés, se podría reestructurar la oración para que la preposición no esté al final, pero suena más formal y menos natural:

For whom did you do it?

En resumen, en la oración "Who did you do it for?", la preposición "for" se coloca al final para mantener un estilo más coloquial y natural en inglés, algo que difiere de la estructura gramatical en español.

Who do you do it for? - ¿Para quién lo haces?
What do you do it for? - ¿Para qué lo haces?
