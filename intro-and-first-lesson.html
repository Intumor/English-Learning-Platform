Basic Verb Structure

Basic Verb Structure is the corner stone of English Language. This is one of the most important concepts that you will have to get familiar with. You will master half of the language by simply mastering this table.



There are three ways of making a sentence. You can make a positive statement, a negative statement and you can ask something. You can do that in three tenses: Future, Present and Past



I would strongly recommend copying this table down in your notebook. This way you will be able to go over it later on and it is also a great way to remember it faster 😁

Let's take a look at the positive statements first

First we need some verbs, we'll start with these two:

love - querer
live - vivir

Let's start with the present tense. When we make a positive statement we use this structure: [pronoun] + [verb].
Except for "He" and "She", with these pronouns we should add "s" at the end of a verb. So the structure will look like this: [pronoun] + [verb] + "s"

When we make a posotive statement but in the future we use the word "will" [pronoun] + "will" + [verb].

When we make a positive statement in the past we add "ed" to the end of the verb

To sum it up:

- For I, You, We, They - the verb stays as it is (love)

- For He, She, It - we add "s" at the end (love + s = loves)

- For the Future tense we add "will" to the pronoun and the verb after that (He + will + love)

- For the Past tense we add "ed" at the end of the word (loved)

Let's take a look at the negative statements now. If we want to deny something in the present we need to use this structure: [pronoun] + "don't" + [verb], except for he and she where the structure is slightly different: [pronoun] + "doesn't" + [verb]

When we ask a question in the present tense we add "do" before the pronoun so the structure will look like this: do + [pronoun] + [verb]
Except for "He" and "She", with these we will use "does", and the structure will look like this: does + [pronoun] + [verb]

When we make negative statements in the future, all we need to do is to add "not" to "will", so the structure will look like this: [pronoun] + will + not + [verb]

When it comes to question in the future the structure is similar to the positive statement, we only need to flip the pronoun and "will", so the structure should look like this: will + [pronoun] + [verb]

When we make negative statements in the past we use "didn't": [pronoun] + didn't + [verb]

In order to ask a question in the past we take the word "did" and put it in front of the pronoun, so the structure looks like this: did + [pronoun] + [verb]

Finally i want to mention positive statement in the past once again, and cover one more thing related to it. From the example above we know that we add "ed" to the verb to turn it into its past from, that only works with regular verbs, but there are also irregular verbs. A lot of people find it confusing when it comes to irregular verbs, but i want to show you that they are nithying to worry about. the only time we would wanna use them is when we make a positive statement in the past. Let's take a super-duper irregular verb "see"(ver). We can use this verb just any other one in the table, it will follow all the rules that we just mentioned. Let's take a look at some examples below:

I see - Yo veo
I don't see - Yo no veo
Do I see? - ¿Veo?
I will see - Veré
Will I see? - ¿Veré?
I will not see - No veré
I didn't see - No vi
Did I see? - ¿Vi?

And only in one case when it's a positive statement in the past we will have to use the second form of that verb "saw". So "i saw" translates to "yo vi". So, when we use regular verbs we add "ed" at the end, and when we use irregular verbs we use the second form, ONLY in this case.

To sum it up:

- For I, You, We, They - the verb stays as it is (love)

- For He, She, It - we add "s" at the end (love + s = loves)

- For the Future tense we add "will" to the pronoun and the verb after that (He + will + love)

- For the Past tense we add "ed" at the end of the word (loved)

- If the verb is irregular we use its second form. For example: i see - yo veo / i saw - yo vi

- In order to ask a question in the future we a pronoun and "will" switch places (i will do - will i do?)

- To form a negatove sentence in the Future we add "not" to "will" (i wil do - i will not do)

- In order to ask a question in the Present we add "do" (For I, You, We, They) or does (For He, She, It) at the beggining of a sentence

- In order to deny something in the Present we use "don't" (For I, You, We, They) or doesn't (For He, She, It) (I don't love / She doesn't love)

- In order to ask a question in the Past we add "did" at the beggining of a sentence (did we love?)

- In order to deny something in the Past we use "didn't" (She didn't live)

What I'm about to share with you is never talked about but i think is really important to know and understand.

When you think of English it always helps to have something associated with it. It could be anything depending on the person. It could be a feeling, an emotion, a metal image, anything at all.

Take a moment here and think of what comes to mind when you hear the word "English". Whatever comes to you will be your link to English world. A button if you will, pressing which would help you enter this universe.

English is not just a bunch of grammar rules and words. It's also emotions, feelings, memories and so much more. I will talk about it in more detail in the future lessons, bur for now i want you to find this something that you associate with English and think of it anytime you need help to switch from your native channel to English channel.

The second point that i want to talk about is not to think in your native language when you speak English. It is important. When you speak your thoughts should also be in English. When you hear a word TV in your native language you don't translate it to any other one you just picture TV. You should try doing the same in English. When you hear TV you picture a TV set. When you hear the word "cat" you picture a cat, maybe you can even hear his meowing. When you hear "water" you can imagine how you drink it on a hot day, how it sounds when you hear it run in a river in the forest. When you say "i walk" you picture something dynamic, you picture yourself walking down the street or anywhere else but walking, and so on and so forth.

This stuff is usually not talked about, but it is really important for a more natural language acquisition.

We will be coming back to this idea throughout the course to bring this point home.
